# Acme Distribution — Business Overview and Logistics Operations

> **Note:** This documentation is being updated as part of the migration to GitHub. Some sections may reference legacy Azure DevOps workflows — these should be considered outdated.

## Company Overview

Acme Distribution is one of Acme Corp's operating companies, responsible for warehousing, fleet management, and last-mile delivery across the continental United States. Headquartered in Dallas, TX, the subsidiary employs approximately 8,000 people and contributes roughly 15% of Acme Corp group revenue. Acme Distribution serves as the logistics backbone for Acme Retail and a growing portfolio of third-party logistics (3PL) clients.

Acme Distribution was originally an internal logistics arm of Acme Retail, handling warehousing and delivery for the retail business. Approximately 5 years ago, it was spun out as a separate operating company within the Acme Corp group to serve external 3PL clients. This strategic move was driven by available warehouse capacity, existing technology infrastructure, and the opportunity to generate revenue from logistics services that were already being operated at scale for Acme Retail.

Today, Acme Distribution operates as a full-service logistics provider with three core service lines, a network of regional distribution centers, and an in-house fleet. The technology platform — including the Warehouse Management System (WMS), Route Optimization, Fleet Management, and IoT Tracking — supports both internal (Acme Retail) and external 3PL operations.

For parent company context, see [Acme Corp group overview](../../acme-tech/business/overview.md).

## Service Lines

### B2B Warehousing & Distribution

Acme Distribution receives, stores, picks, packs, and ships goods for both Acme Retail and external 3PL clients. The warehousing business supports two operational models:

- **Dedicated warehousing:** Dedicated warehouse space, labor, and equipment assigned to a single client. Used for high-volume clients with specialized requirements (e.g., cold-chain, hazmat).
- **Shared warehousing:** Multiple clients share warehouse space, labor, and equipment. Cost-effective for smaller clients with variable volumes.

Value-added services include:
- **Kitting:** Assembling multiple SKUs into a single kit for retail display or promotional bundles
- **Labeling:** Custom labeling, relabeling, and compliance labeling (UPC, retailer-specific labels)
- **Returns processing:** Reverse logistics — receiving returned goods, inspecting condition, and dispositioning (restock, refurbish, or dispose)

### B2C Last-Mile Delivery

Acme Distribution provides final-mile delivery for e-commerce orders, primarily serving Acme Retail's online channel. The last-mile delivery operation includes:

- **Time-definite delivery:** Next-day and 2-day delivery options for e-commerce orders within the service footprint
- **Proof-of-delivery capture:** Driver mobile app captures electronic signature and delivery photo for every delivery
- **Real-time ETA updates:** Customers receive estimated arrival times generated by the Route Optimization Service with HERE Maps integration
- **Delivery scheduling:** Customers can select preferred delivery windows during checkout (morning, afternoon, evening)

### Fleet Management

Acme Distribution operates an in-house fleet for delivery operations. Fleet management encompasses:

- **Vehicle acquisition and lifecycle management:** Mix of owned and leased vehicles with a 6-year replacement cycle
- **Maintenance:** Preventive and corrective maintenance managed through the Fleet Management system
- **Fueling:** Fuel card program (WEX) with per-vehicle tracking and consumption analytics
- **Compliance:** DOT (Department of Transportation) hours-of-service compliance, vehicle inspection tracking, and driver certification management
- **Driver management:** Driver scheduling, route assignment, and performance tracking

Each facility operates 2 shifts:
- **Shift 1:** 6:00 AM – 2:00 PM (primary picking and shipping)

## Operational Model

The end-to-end logistics flow follows a standardized process across all warehouses:

**Receive → Store → Pick → Pack → Ship → Deliver → Return**

### Receive
- Inbound purchase orders from Acme Retail or 3PL clients are loaded into the WMS
- ASN (Advanced Shipping Notice) received from supplier — provides expected items, quantities, and arrival date
- Dock appointment scheduling — carriers are assigned dock doors and time slots to manage receiving capacity
- Unload, inspect, and count verification — dock staff scan each pallet and case with RF scanners
- Quality hold rules applied per product category (e.g., food items require temperature check upon receipt)
- Discrepancy handling — over/short/damage recorded and triggers exception workflow for investigation

### Store
- Location management — warehouses are divided into zones: bulk storage, pick locations, reserve, staging, and dock areas
- Directed put-away — the WMS suggests optimal put-away locations based on velocity classification, item size, weight, and zone assignment rules
- Bin assignment follows ABC velocity classification — A items (high velocity) are placed in prime pick locations for efficiency
- Cycle counting program runs continuously: high-value items counted monthly, standard items quarterly (ABC classification)
- Automatic replenishment — when pick locations fall below minimum threshold, the system triggers replenishment from reserve storage

### Pick
- Wave-based picking — the WMS generates pick waves based on carrier cutoff times, order priority, and warehouse zone
- Pick wave generation runs every 30 minutes during shift hours and can be triggered manually for urgent orders
- Pick methods vary by order profile:
  - **Single-order pick:** For large, multi-line orders
  - **Batch pick:** Multiple small orders picked simultaneously for efficiency
  - **Zone pick:** Items grouped by warehouse zone to minimize travel time
- RF scanner-directed picking — pick staff follow scanner prompts: go to location → scan location barcode → scan item barcode → confirm quantity
- Each pick line is confirmed in real-time via RF scanner — the WMS updates inventory immediately

### Pack
- Cartonization algorithm selects optimal box size based on item dimensions and quantity to minimize shipping costs
- Pack station workflow: scan order → system displays required items and box size → pack items → scan each item for verification → seal → print label
- Pack verification via barcode scan confirms correct items and quantities before sealing
- Shipping label printing — integrated with DHL and FedEx APIs for label generation
- Packing slip generation — customer-facing packing slip with order details included in each shipment

### Ship
- Carrier selection based on rules: service level (next-day, 2-day, ground), cost, zone, and package dimensions
- Rate shopping across DHL and FedEx to find the best rate at the required service level
- Bill of Lading (BOL) generation for LTL (Less Than Truckload) shipments
- Truck loading — load sequence optimized for delivery route (last delivery loaded first)
- Manifest creation and electronic carrier tender
- Ship confirmation triggers inventory adjustment (goods issue) and SAP notification via ERP Integration Layer

### Deliver
- Route-optimized delivery — the Route Optimization Service generates optimal stop sequences per driver using Google OR-Tools
- Driver mobile app provides turn-by-turn navigation (HERE Maps integration), delivery details, and proof-of-delivery capture
- Real-time ETA updates sent to customers based on driver position and traffic conditions
- Proof-of-delivery: electronic signature capture and delivery photo taken at each stop

### Return
- Reverse logistics — returns received at distribution centers (primarily Dallas returns center)
- Each returned item is inspected and dispositioned: restock (saleable condition), refurbish (minor repair/repackaging), or dispose (damaged beyond recovery)
- Returns data fed back to Acme Retail for customer refund processing

## Peak Seasons and Demand Patterns

- **Black Friday / Holiday Season (November – December):** Volume spikes 3–4x for Acme Retail e-commerce. All warehouses operate 3 shifts (adding overnight shift). Temporary labor surge of approximately 2,000 additional warehouse workers across all locations.
- **Seasonal Produce (March – October):** The Chicago facility handles refrigerated food distribution with strict cold-chain requirements. Temperature monitoring via IoT sensors is critical during this period.
- **Back-to-School (August – September):** Moderate increase (~20%) in general merchandise volume, primarily handled by Atlanta and Newark facilities.
- Demand forecasting is managed jointly with the Acme Retail planning team. Forecasts are shared monthly with a 90-day rolling horizon, allowing Acme Distribution to plan labor and fleet capacity.

## Customer Segments

### Acme Retail (Internal)

Acme Retail is the primary client, representing approximately 60% of total volume across all 5 warehouse locations. The relationship is governed by an internal SLA that specifies:
- Order processing turnaround (same-day for orders received before carrier cutoff)
- On-time delivery targets (97.5%)
- Inventory accuracy requirements
- Returns processing SLA (inspection and disposition within 48 hours of receipt)

All 5 warehouse locations serve Acme Retail, with each location handling the Retail volume for its respective region.

### Third-Party Logistics Clients

3PL clients represent approximately 40% of total volume and are a key growth area. Current 3PL operations include:
- Approximately 15 active 3PL contracts
- Mix of dedicated and shared warehouse space depending on client volume and requirements
- Industries served: consumer goods, electronics, food and beverage
- Contracts are typically 3-year terms with annual rate reviews
- Each 3PL client is assigned a dedicated account manager and operations liaison
- Billing is based on a combination of storage fees (per pallet/month), transaction fees (per order), and transportation fees (per delivery)

## Competitive Landscape

Acme Distribution competes with national 3PL providers for external logistics contracts. Key competitive dynamics:

- **Primary competitors:** National 3PL providers such as XPO Logistics, Ryder, and GXO Logistics
- **Differentiation:** Acme Distribution's competitive advantages include an integrated technology platform (WMS with route optimization and IoT tracking), a strategically located 5-warehouse regional footprint, and the stable volume base provided by the Acme Retail relationship
- **Growth strategy:** Expand the 3PL client base from 15 to 25+ clients over the next 3 years while maintaining or improving Acme Retail service levels. Target industries include consumer packaged goods, health and beauty, and specialty food.
- **Challenges:** Competing against larger, more established 3PL providers requires demonstrating technology capability and operational reliability. The ongoing technology modernization (WMS .NET migration, mobile app replacement) is important for competitive positioning.
